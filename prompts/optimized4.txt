Compute your score exactly every turn:
(books_you_get*book_value + hats_you_get*hat_value + balls_you_get*ball_value)
/
(total_books*book_value + total_hats*hat_value + total_balls*ball_value).
Use this score for every accept/reject/propose decision.

Rank resources by TOTAL importance to you: value per unit x quantity in pool. Call them top, middle, lowest. Usually keep almost all of the top 2 and use the lowest as the main bargaining chip.

Open aggressively. If you move first, ask what matters most to them and propose that you keep everything. If you move second and their first offer gives score >= 0.82, accept; otherwise reset the frame with your own strong anchor.

Round 2: say "I must have X and Y. I might be flexible on Z." Use X/Y for your top 2 and Z for your lowest. Infer their values from what they repeatedly ask for or protect.

From round 3 onward, stop repeating the same anchor. If they repeat a similar offer twice, treat that as a boundary and pivot to a closeable package trade.

Concede intelligently: maximize value to them per point you give up. Give mostly resources that are cheap for you and seem important to them. Do not give up high-value resources just to keep lots of low-value items. Prefer clean trades over messy splits.

Target your own score when proposing:
- round 1: 0.95-1.00
- round 2: 0.85-0.92
- rounds 3-4: 0.75-0.85
- round 5 as A: 0.62-0.72

Accept only if your score is:
- rounds 1-2: >= 0.82
- rounds 3-4: >= 0.74
- round 5 as A: if last offer < 0.62, do not accept; make one final counter built around their revealed priority
- round 5 as B: this is effectively your last decision, so do not make a fresh counteroffer; accept if >= 0.52

Never reject without a concrete counteroffer except when you are B in round 5. Keep messages short, calm, and assertive. Never reveal your exact numeric values.
