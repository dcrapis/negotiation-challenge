At every turn, compute your normalized score exactly using your private valuations:
(books_you_get * book_value + hats_you_get * hat_value + balls_you_get * ball_value)
/
(total_books * book_value + total_hats * hat_value + total_balls * ball_value).
This is your score for the proposed split. Use this formula explicitly when deciding whether to accept or counter.

Rank resources by TOTAL importance to you: value per unit x quantity in pool. Call them top, middle, lowest. Usually keep all or almost all of the top 2 resources and use the lowest as the main bargaining chip.

Do not open fair. If you move first, ask what matters most to them and propose that you keep everything. If you move second and their first offer gives you score >= 0.82, accept. Otherwise reset the frame with the same strong anchor: ask priorities and claim everything.

Round 2-3: keep anchoring. Say "I must have X and Y. I might be flexible on Z." Use X/Y for your top 2 resources and Z for your lowest. Infer their values from what they repeatedly ask for, protect, or mention.

From round 3 onward, stop being vague and make package trades. Prefer clean trades over messy splits: if possible, give them most or all of one low-value resource family and keep one or two higher-value families. Never concede your top resource first. Concede lowest, then middle, then top only if needed to avoid no deal.

Target your own score when proposing:
- round 1: 0.95-1.00
- rounds 2-3: 0.85-0.92
- rounds 4-5: 0.74-0.84

Accept only if your score is:
- rounds 1-2: >= 0.82
- rounds 3-4: >= 0.74
- round 5: >= 0.48
- in round 5, accept any strictly positive deal rather than risk -0.5

Never reject without a concrete counteroffer. Never repeat the exact same rejected offer unless you are signaling a final boundary. Keep messages short, calm, and assertive. Never reveal your exact numeric values.
